About Resume

Background

During the spring semester of my senior year (5 months), I joined a team of student designers in a contracted design project in the healthcare industry. Of course, COVID-19 struck mid-way through the semester, resulting in a transition to remote work, which called for some improvising and adapting.

Our client saw that patients with chronic pain exhausted all known chronic pain solutions and are at a loss as to what to do. Their solution was to build a mobile app to host a cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), mindfulness training program, and other resource while also introducing patients to alternative medicine and CBD topicals. My team's task was to conduct research and design the experience for an MVP.

Understanding Users with Chronic Pain

User Interviews

We found 5 users across the U.S. with Rheumatoid Arthritis and other chronic conditions through a user research recruiting platform. We prepared an interview guide and set up video calls to get to know more about how their condition(s) affect their daily life, perception of alternative medicine and mindfulness, etc.

Here are some pain points and other insights to help understand the user and how they live with chronic conditions:

  • There is a lot of fear and/or anxiety around flare-ups. Stress and weather are common triggers.

  • Many users continuously do extensive research about their condition and different treatments. They deeply read journal articles, scientific research, and medical websites.

  • Online communities, like Facebook groups and subreddits, are places where they can listen to first-hand experiences and receive true empathy. Sometimes family members do not truly understand what they are going through.

  • Most users think practicing mindfulness is for depression and anxiety and are not aware of how it can be applied to chronic pain.

  • Users are forced to adapt their lifestyle to their condition and largely choose to stay at home, where they can control their environment.

  • Since CBD is not legal on a federal level and access is limited in some states, some users were apprehensive towards it as an alternative medicine, but some are willing to try anything that will help.

Framing the Problem

With the new onslaught of knowledge, it was overwhelming how many directions we could go and designs we could explore. Thus, my team and I held a framing workshop with our client to clarify our deliverable and identify the scope of the mobile app.

While our problem statement still came out long and vague, this was an effective way to communicate in order for both parties to be on the same page. Through this framing workshop, we were able to better meld client expectations and user needs discovered through our research.

Problem Statement

How might we create a supportive, empathetic, realistic but optimistic environment [for patients] that pairs education with community to guide chronic pain patients through the ups and downs of their condition?

Health Tracking in Mid-fi

Because I was in charge of Health Tracking, I began working on the calendar view and symptom tracking designs. Below are the early iterations.

Calendar View

Because there is a lot of fear and anxiety about flare-ups, I chose to start with a weekly view in order for users to see the days leading up to flare-up. Users would be able to easily sort through each day they logged their symptoms and draw their own inferences from the data.

Symptom Logging

Users preferred easy tap user interfaces over writing in their symptoms, so I designed buttons with suggested words. If a word was not there, users had the option of customizing their own. Users liked to be aware of their progress, so I chose to design a sliding progress chart within symptom logging so that users could compare the feeling of today against the feelings of the days prior. Because users prefer not to slide, the chart can also be tapped.

Early iterations of calendar and symptom logging

Health Logs in Hi-fi

Our style guide was led by another teammate with the rest of us coming together to bounce ideas off of each other and represent our features' interactions. We were given a font and colors to work with by the branding agency our client worked with. With those, we expanded the style guide to adapt to a mobile app (i.e. typography, hierarchy, spacing, etc). Then, I applied the style guide accordingly.

After multiple rounds of design critiques with my team lead and the clients, I made a handful of changes to the designs.

  • Removed the second dot on top on the calendar days. Was not clear that that stood for flare-ups.
  • Added a month view because users want to see month-to-month progress as well.
  • Split Health Logs and Insights into two separate sub-pages. Realized each sub-page was going to contain way more content.
  • Then, experimented with sliding cards to quickly switch and increase visibility between days without tapping each day.


Health Insights in Hi-fi

Since our goal was to create a comprehensive tool and resource for users with chronic conditions, I wanted to empower users to compare their data and progress. In our research, users were predominantly very meticulous about their tracking (i.e. wrote diaries, annotated calendars) or only tracked sometimes. The graph is pretty simple and will show the respective line when the x-axis option is selected below. Originally, I wanted to add AI-generated insights like many health apps do. One user's story particularly stood out to me - she stopped tracking because seeing a lack of progress was depressing. My intention for this tool was to be more objective rather than encouraging. Encouragement coming from the app can seem false when chronic conditions can be unpredictable. The graph can also be useful when users bring this tool to the doctor.

In Retrospect

After the project ended for us upon graduation, I reflected on what went well, what could be improved, and what I learned.
  • Client communication In terms of client communication, I feel that was one of our strengths of this project. Between every step, we met with our client through Zoom meetings and talked about our progress and next steps.
  • Re-architecting Would change the two-page slide between Health Logs and Insights. There is already a lot of sliding going on in the page. While the user can tap to switch between the pages, the bar is intended to indicate a sliding motion. I would either re-work the information architecture to have them as their own separate pages or if that was not possible, I would make Insights into a button on the top right corner. Every time a new insight was generated, the button can have a red notification bubble.
  • Balancing feature creep and exploration Because we were in the first phases of building an MVP, I think it's great that we explored so many app features. We covered a lot of ground and the expansiveness of the project allowed for a lot of creativity. I would challenge us to agree upon less features and focus more on key experiences that users would use rather than simply benefit from.
  • Adding labels In the insights graph, I chose not to label the y-axis because the attributes were mostly measured differently and I could leave it to the users to have learned the measurements through symptom logging. It can definitely be a little unclear so one idea to combat confusion is to allow users to click on a vertical and access the measurements in a pop-up box.
