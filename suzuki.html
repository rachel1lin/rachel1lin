Background

In partnership with Suzuki Motors, our Berkeley Innovation team conducted research about Gen Z car shopping habits to shape Suzuki’s strategy for re-entering the U.S. market and winning the international car scene. As technology progresses, so do shopping habits. Our final solution was to reach Gen Z through a chatbot that educated users and diverted consumer behavior towards the Suzuki online car catalogue.

Our final deliverables were a chatbot prototype made through Chatfuel and Facebook Messenger and a research brief to pass on to Suzuki executives.

User Problem

Future shopping habits are changing and Gen Z is adapting and adopting new ways to consume.

Opportunity

Build and test emerging technologies that cater towards Gen Z needs.

Research

I was the Sprint Leader in charge of leading research activities and data collection methods.

Because we were starting with an ambiguous ask, I wanted to use a variety of research methods to gauge Gen Z shopping habits. We first consulted secondary research for studies already done on Gen Z and for me, I knew very little about cars in general so this step was especially helpful for me. I also led my team in doing empathy practices, framing workshop, Qualtrics survey, and user interivews. This was an ambitious amount of research to be done so I extended our sprint to be longer than the typical 2 weeks.

Insights

Here are a handful of insights from our survey and interviews that led us to create a chatbot. We decided to focus on the car research portion of the buying experience due to feasibility and the prevalence of pain points there.
  • Respondents’ knowledge about cars is very varied, and knowledge is generally pretty low.
  • 62% of respondents do not know where to sell a car.
  • 91% would choose to visit a dealership, 9% would buy a car online, and 0% would buy from a vending machine.
  • 90% plan on buying a car in the future; 10% do not plan to buy a car.
  • For those planning to buy a car, majority plan to buy it at age 22-26.
  • Respondents prefer cars because they have full autonomy over where they can go, cars are more convenient, and public transportation is unreliable.

User Testing

We asked 8 users to try out the chatbot flow and gave them the same prompt. I would consider the prototype successful if users could finish the given task, enjoyed using the product, and drew helpful information from it. Here are the insights from the user testing sessions:
  • Suzi's casual tone was generally positively received. Most found her to be reliable and trustworthy though one user felt the opposite.
  • Some users felt Suzi offered the same information they could find through Google. They would prefer if the chatbot could craft a more tailored response.
  • Users were initially unfamiliar with chatbots, but quickly became comfortable with the chatbot format and encountered few usability issues.

In Retrospect

  • The dialog tested moves linearly like a conversation, which worked as users were only given one flow to go through. However, my concern is how users will continue to discover content. If users do not rely on typing in questions and instead use the given buttons to navigate, then users will encounter more friction scrolling up to find the prompt they're looking for. Ideally, we would not use Chatfuel and design our own.
  • I'd like to explore more of how this chatbot delivers information. I would design it so it gives a couple of bite-size information messages and allows the user to react in between instead of how it is now, where it seems very one-sided but has potential for more personality. So far, the prototype is functional but not social.
  • Another feature I found helpful through Facebook Messenger chatbots was when the chatbot would send me daily or weekly notifications of car facts and opportunities to learn more. I would like to add that to the onboarding portion of the chatbot and allow users to choose how often they wanted notifications.
