When I joined Cisco this summer, the Cisco Customer Experience Team was working on the Customer Portal (CP), an application that consolidates and organizes the resources for all Cisco products. Clients have struggled in using Ciscoâ€™s antiquated search function to troubleshoot problems, and opening a support case can be costly and inefficient. The CP provides a step-by-step guide for all Cisco products and filters the relevant resources (PDFs, webinars, license training, etc.) for each step. It allows clients to proactively prevent problems instead of retroactively scrambling to fix, say, a network crash.

My project involved validating and designing the mobile app version of the CP.

IDEATION


My ideation session consisted of looking at the web CPâ€™s interactive prototype, messily sketching some screen ideas (top half), and writing out all the possible tasks (bottom half). The web CP did not have a home page yet, so I sketched a possible home screen with click-through notifications and a hamburger menu for navigation.

There were a number of possible flows to explore, and we ambitiously wanted to do all of them. Instead of making a hundred screens, we crafted a user story that encapsulated the values of a mobile app and focused on two different user journeys, split between my collaborator and me.

My user journey involved the Home Page and Lifecycle features.

USER JOURNEY + PROTOTYPING


The user journey demonstrates the different touch points where an engineer will interact with his/her phone.

Ned is a network engineer whose company has acquired a new site, and he is responsible for setting up its network. He wants to do a thorough job and knows that the CP will allow him to take preventative measures and maintain a healthy network. So begins his user journey with the CP.

The first thing he does is schedule a webinar from his laptop while at work to learn about setting up his product. Nedâ€™s days are filled with meetings and collaboration, so he wants to take his learning outside the workplace. The scheduled webinar shows up in the app's home screen and from there, he watches the webinar on the commute to-and-from work, thanks to the portability of his mobile phone.

In this demo, Ned goes walks through the experience above (without watching the video) and browses through the suggested resources. My team wanted to further drive engagement after a video was watched in order for the app to bring more value to the user. Thus, I added the "Suggested Resources" section, so that the user experience could cascade through more and more resources. This demo includes the experience of scheduling another webinar.
The web-based portal didn't have a home page, so I added an initial home screen page that allows for Ned to see the most important and relevant information right when he opens the app, a design decision driven by our research. I originally designed a drag-and-drop list to help users sort their priorities because users said they valued customizability in a mobile app, but this design would limit the amount of content that could be displayed. I decided to switch customizability for cleanliness and employed the Star filter, inspired by the Twitter and Gmail mobile apps. This method works well with a larger number of notifications and content can be more efficiently sorted with the Starred tab.
Coming back to the original story, Ned needs to go to the network site to set up his product. He uses his computer to pull up the command line interface but needs the instructions. Luckily, he carries his phone with him wherever he goes. He goes through the mobile app again to the Lifecycle page, where he finds the relevant PDF for setting up his product, as demo'd below. Ned successfully uses his phone and computer in parallel to efficiently and correctly set up his network hardware.

POSSIBLE NEXT STEPS


While the Lifecycle pages are a translation from the current web CP prototype, the home page is a new endeavor. If I could continue on this project, I would want to do user testing on the home page to see what kind of format/functions users would prefer e.g. to-do list, drag-and-drop, deleting highlights, etc.

NOTES


Our reasoning from the research initially led us to believe that mobile notifications were the most important feature that the mobile CP could provide. One of our stakeholders pointed out that users could simply get an email about any important updates. I realized that just because users expected notifications, it didnâ€™t mean that notifications would definitely bring any value to the user. Thus, I reevaluated our research and focused on the use case that highlighted the portability and convenience of a mobile app.

Another designer asked us if the CP could simply be opened in a web browser rather than a mobile app. My answer to that is that a mobile app allows for a better UI, especially because the web CP has so much information. A web browser has limited functionalities that would not allow for future AR and WebEx integrations and filtered home page.
